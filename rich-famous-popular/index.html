  • Software Carpentry now has 1000 instructors in 35 countries

How can I figure out what's worth doing?

  1. It will help people who actually need help.
    • Including you.
  2. You will enjoy doing the work.
  3. You will learn things that might change how you see the world.
  4. You will get to meet and work with people whom you like and respect.
  5. People will say, "Cool!" when you tell them about it.

How can I figure out what's not worth doing?

  1. There are already lots of books on the subject.
    • Yours will have to be pretty good to get attention.
  2. There's an active community doing it.
    • Join that community!
  3. Your closest friends are politely noncommittal when they read your one-paragraph pitch.
    • Most of your ideas won't be good ones.
    • But they'll still give you an excuse to talk to your friends.

A few ideas

  • I keep mine at http://third-bit.com/ideas/.
  • I'd be overjoyed to see other people implement these.
    • Life is short.

Sex and Drugs and Guns and Code: What Everyone in Tech Needs to Know About Politics, Economics, and Power

  • Inspired by Physics for Future Presidents and The Imposter's Handbook.
  • Accessible introduction to ideas, methods, and findings from the social sciences.
  • Shows that "the way things are" is neither inevitable nor accidental.
  • See http://third-bit.com/reading/ for a reading list.

The Undergraduate Operator's Manual

  • Textbook for an engaging one-semester course on physiology, psychology, and organizational behavior.
  • What effect does pulling an all-nighter have on the quality of your work?
  • How do people absorb and retain knowledge?
  • What are some good ways to run a project meeting?
  • What are early signs of depression and what can you do about it?

Software Tools in JavaScript

  • Update to the classic Software Tools in Pascal.
  • Then: imperative Pascal, ASCII text, and standard I/O.
  • Now: mostly-functional JavaScript, JSON, and HTTP.
  • Use calendar aggregation as a running example.
  • And "Seven Ways to Think Like the Web" as inspiration.

Research Computing from A to B

  • Based on Software Carpentry, Data Carpentry, Library Carpentry, and Programming Historian
  • Hands-on introduction to practical computing skills for graduate students and professionals in research-intensive disciplines.
  • "From spreadsheets to continuous integration."

Software Engineering: An Evidence-Based Approach

  • How to get, clean up, model, analyze, and make sense of software engineering data.
  • How to use software analysis tools.
  • How to think sceptically about claims and evidence in software development.

Software Architecture by Example

  • Compare and contrast key features of equivalent open source programs.
  • E.g., where do the request processing cycles of Apache and Nginx differ and why?
  • Teach programmers how to make sense of code.
  • Show them how other people have approached design challenges.

Teach programmers how to organize

  • How to start and run community organizations like get-into-coding groups
  • Start with Building Powerful Community Organizations

Browsercast

  • A tool to create synchronized voiceovers for HTML slideshows.
  • Much smaller payload for mobile devices.
  • Plays well with search engines and accessibility aids.

Diff and merge for common document formats

  • Branch-and-merge is a scalable way to collaborate.
  • But today's tools don't support Word, Excel, and PowerPoint.
  • Give millions of people a ramp instead of a cliff.

The Discussion Book online

  • 50 productive ways for groups to discuss things and make decisions.
  • Only a dozen have ever been implemented online.

Using machine learning to find actual design patterns

  • Analyze novice programs to find nameable (teachable) patterns.
  • Do the same for actual usage patterns in Git.

Numerical JavaScript

  • Sooner or later, every programmer has to learn some JavaScript.
  • So give it numerical extensions instead of creating entirely new languages.
